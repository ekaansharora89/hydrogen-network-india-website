Why India's Electrolysis Plants Run on Alkaline — Not PEM

A detailed cost breakdown, technology comparison, and the strategic logic behind India's ₹1.85 lakh crore green hydrogen bet.

📍 India · Green Hydrogen ⏱ 8 min read 📅 21 February 2025
The Opening Question

The Question Everyone in Energy is Asking

India has committed to producing 5 million tonnes of green hydrogen annually by 2030. Electrolyzers sit at the heart of this mission. So why, despite PEM's technological appeal, is alkaline technology winning on nearly every major project across the country?

Walk into any large-scale green hydrogen project in India — L&T's plant in Hazira, Reliance's gigafactory in Jamnagar, Advait Energy's SECI-backed capacity, or NTPC's pilot programs — and the answer becomes clear. It's not about ignoring innovation. It's about ruthless economic pragmatism at scale.

This article breaks it all down — the numbers, the engineering tradeoffs, the India-specific context, and what the future looks like as both technologies race to cost parity.

The Technology

How They Work: A Quick Primer

Alkaline Electrolyzers (AEL) split water using a liquid electrolyte — typically potassium hydroxide (KOH) dissolved in water. Two nickel-based electrodes are separated by a porous diaphragm. This technology has been around for over 100 years and has powered industrial hydrogen production globally since the 1920s.

PEM Electrolyzers (PEMEL) use a solid polymer membrane — the proton exchange membrane — as the electrolyte. Water is fed to the anode side, where it splits into protons, electrons, and oxygen. Protons cross the membrane and combine with electrons at the cathode to form hydrogen. The membrane requires platinum and iridium as catalysts — both precious metals.

The India Factor

Why Alkaline Wins in India's Specific Context

🇮🇳 Five India-Specific Reasons Alkaline Dominates

1. Supply Chain Advantage: Nickel-based electrodes and steel bipolar plates can be manufactured domestically. India has steel at scale. Iridium — PEM's critical catalyst — is one of the rarest elements on Earth, found primarily in South Africa and Russia. India imports 100% of it.

2. SIGHT Scheme Incentives: Under the National Green Hydrogen Mission's SIGHT scheme, SECI has allocated manufacturing capacity to players like Advait Energy (200 MW alkaline) and L&T (300 MW alkaline). These allocations signal regulatory confidence in alkaline as India's primary scale-up technology.

3. Baseload Power Profile: India's large-scale green hydrogen projects — for ammonia, urea, methanol — require steady, high-capacity production. Alkaline's strength is exactly here: stable, baseload operation with grid-tied renewable power. India's RE mix (70%+ solar) provides relatively stable generation curves in peak hours.

4. Local Engineering Talent: Alkaline electrolysis is well-understood by Indian engineers. Maintenance, troubleshooting, and BOP integration draw on decades of industrial chemical engineering knowledge already present in the country.

5. Pure Capital Cost: At a ₹1.85 lakh crore hub like NTPC's Pudimadaka project (7 GW electrolyzer capacity), even a 20% CAPEX difference between alkaline and PEM translates to savings of tens of thousands of crores. At this scale, economics are existential.

Pros & Cons

Alkaline Electrolyzer: The Full Picture

✓ Advantages
  • Lower CAPEX — 30–40% cheaper than PEM at current market prices
  • Proven at large scale — 100+ MW installations globally
  • No precious metals — nickel-based electrodes are affordable and locally sourceable
  • Long system lifetime — 20–30 years vs PEM's 10–22
  • 80%+ indigenisation potential in India (CEEW 2024)
  • Well-understood maintenance — large talent pool available
  • New "pressurized alkaline 2.0" designs bring improved dynamic response
✗ Limitations
  • Lower current density (0.2–0.7 A/cm²) means larger physical footprint
  • 2–3× the floor space requirement vs PEM for same output
  • Slower cold-start: 20–60 min vs PEM's 5–15 min
  • Minimum load of 20–40% — poor flexibility with highly variable renewables
  • KOH electrolyte is corrosive — specialized handling, disposal costs
  • Lower hydrogen purity — additional purification steps may be needed
  • Low output pressure (1–10 bar) requires downstream compression

PEM Electrolyzer: The Full Picture

✓ Advantages
  • Rapid response — ideal for variable renewable (wind/solar) inputs
  • Minimum load as low as 3% — exceptional flexibility
  • High output pressure (30–80 bar) reduces downstream compression costs
  • Compact design — 2–3× smaller footprint than alkaline for equal output
  • High hydrogen purity — suitable for fuel cells and precision applications
  • Lower liquid chemical handling — water-only input is safer
  • O&M costs trending toward alkaline-level parity by 2030
✗ Limitations
  • 15–30% higher CAPEX than alkaline at current pricing
  • Iridium catalyst — rare, expensive, 100% imported in India
  • Nafion membrane — proprietary, expensive, no domestic Indian supply
  • Shorter stack life — more frequent replacement cycles
  • Limited large-scale field data — biggest deployment is ~20 MW
  • Specialized maintenance — smaller talent pool in India
  • Membrane degradation accelerates with dynamic load cycling

Who's Doing What in India

The Players & Their Technology Choices

L&T Electrolysers — Commissioned India's first indigenous 1 MW electrolyzer at Hazira, Gujarat in March 2024. Technology: pressurized alkaline, developed with France-based McPhy Energy. Allocated 300 MW/year under SIGHT. Now building a Panipat facility for large-scale green ammonia.

Reliance Industries — Signed a technology licensing agreement with Norway's Nel ASA in May 2024 to manufacture alkaline electrolyzers in India at its Jamnagar gigafactory. Also partnered with Denmark's Stiesdal for HydroGen electrolyzers. Betting big on alkaline for green ammonia and refinery hydrogen.

Advait Energy Transitions — Secured a 200 MW alkaline electrolyzer manufacturing capacity under SECI's SIGHT scheme. Licensed Chinese Guofu Hydrogen's alkaline technology. Plans to scale to 1,000 MW/year.

NTPC (via NGEL) — Running pilots across all four major electrolyzer types (alkaline, PEM, SOE, AEM). The 7 GW Pudimadaka hub — India's first Green Hydrogen Hub — will be the real proof point. NTPC already uses alkaline electrolyzers in its coal plants for generator cooling — the institutional familiarity is real.

BPCL — Partnered with BARC to develop domestic alkaline electrolyzer technology for refinery hydrogen. A strategic move to reduce import dependency for a core industrial application.

Ohmium International — The notable PEM player in India, partnered with Tata Projects. Signed an MOU with NTPC for up to 400 MW of PEM deployment. Also exploring fuel cell integration with Toyota Kirloskar. PEM's best use case in India: smaller, dynamic applications, mobility, and precision industrial hydrogen.

The Horizon

Will PEM Close the Gap by 2030?

The honest answer: partially. Here's what the trajectory looks like.

ScienceDirect research projects alkaline stack costs falling from $242–388/kW today to $52–79/kW by 2030. PEM falls from $384–1,071/kW to $63–234/kW. The gap narrows — but doesn't disappear. PEM's learning curve is steeper precisely because its current costs are so high and manufacturing is less mature.

At the system level (including balance of plant), PEM offers an interesting counterpoint: at scales above 10 MW, PEM's BOP costs per kW actually become more competitive because high output pressure reduces downstream compression infrastructure. One analysis from Plug Power suggests PEM's total cost of ownership — including service — could be one-third that of alkaline in some configurations.

After 2030, most new green hydrogen projects will need to ensure an hourly match between power generation and consumption. That's where PEM's dynamic response becomes indispensable.

— BloombergNEF, Electrolyzer Market Outlook 2024

The emerging picture: Alkaline for the 2020s buildout at industrial scale. PEM for post-2030 as off-grid variable renewable projects grow and iridium supply chains improve. India is smart to build alkaline manufacturing now — the cost curve will make it competitive for years — while piloting PEM to build institutional expertise.

The Verdict

Alkaline Today. PEM Tomorrow. Both, Always.

India's electrolyzer strategy is neither ignorant nor ideological. Alkaline wins in 2025 because the economics are clear: lower CAPEX, domestic supply chains, proven technology, 80% indigenisation potential, and fitness for baseload industrial applications. PEM wins in flexibility, purity, and compact design — but those advantages don't yet justify the cost premium at gigawatt scale in an emerging market racing against time.

The companies watching this space should track one number above all others: iridium availability and price. If iridium supply chains expand and Nafion membrane manufacturing comes to India, PEM's tipping point arrives much faster. Until then, the alkaline bet is the right one.

India's ₹1.85 lakh crore Pudimadaka hub, Reliance's Jamnagar gigafactory, and L&T's Hazira plant are all writing the same conclusion in cement and steel: alkaline electrolysis is the foundation of India's green hydrogen decade.
